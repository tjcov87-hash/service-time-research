Hidden Cost 2: On-Road Inefficiency

Even after floor sorting, the van is not perfectly sequenced by zone. Drivers still search the van at each stop (+8.7s) and take sub-optimal routes between stops (86% route efficiency).

$342k/yr · 24.7 min/driver/day
The Solution

Zone pre-sort: the DC does the hard work, not the driver

Sort packages at the DC into tight geographic zones of 15–25 stops, in delivery sequence. Drivers arrive, load their van in 25 minutes, and start delivering immediately. No floor sort. No van searching. No backtracking.

Three-Way Comparison

Current state vs high-capacity van vs zone pre-sort

The high-capacity van alternative does solve the floor sort problem — but replaces it with a 60-minute loading problem, requires $1M+ in fleet capex, and still loses to zone sort by over $1.1M per year.

The key difference: how you sort determines how you load

A high-capacity van sorts to exact sequence at the DC — good. But loading 100+ packages in precise sequence into a large van takes a full hour because every package must be placed in exactly the right position. Zone sort loads 15–25 packages per zone batch — small enough to stage in 25 minutes with no precision placement required.

Key assumptions — and how robust they are

Very low risk
Floor sort happens in current state — directly observed operational behaviour. The 85-minute mean is conservative (modelled as truncated normal with range 30–120 min). This is the dominant saving and carries near-zero model risk.
Low risk
High-cap van load time is 60 min — stated directly by operations. Even if this improves to 45 min with practice, zone sort still wins by $724k/yr on load time alone.
Medium risk
Zone sort load time is 25 min — operationally achievable with small ordered batches, but needs validation in pilot. If it takes 35 min, saving reduces by $104k/yr — still $2.27M total.
Low risk
On-road savings modelled from 205,000 observed stops — log-normal distribution confirmed by AIC comparison. Service time and route efficiency models are statistically robust.

Recommendation

What we recommend

The case is overwhelmingly positive. The floor sort saving alone — the most certain component — exceeds $1.76M per year. A phased pilot de-risks the implementation assumptions before full commitment.

Phase 1 — Pilot
Weeks 1–6
  • →Select Surry Hills + Waterloo (high density, mix of stop types)
  • →Implement zone bins for these areas only
  • →Stopwatch-time actual floor sort to validate 85-min assumption
  • →Measure load time for zone-sort drivers vs control group
  • →Record dwell times at each stop
Phase 2 — Validate & Calibrate
Weeks 7–12
  • →Re-run model with observed floor sort and load times
  • →Confirm zone boundaries from driver feedback
  • →Quantify DC sort productivity uplift required
  • →Build detailed DC process cost for full rollout
Phase 3 — Full Rollout
Months 4–9
  • →Extend to all 13 suburbs, all 694 zones
  • →Track: floor sort time (→ 0), load time, pkgs/hr, dwell time
  • →Re-run model quarterly as volumes and costs change
  • →Expand model to other DCs using same zone framework

Decision summary

Why zone sort, not high-cap van
  • ✓Saves 90 min/driver/day vs high-cap van's 55 min — better time recovery
  • ✓No $1M+ capex required for fleet upgrade
  • ✓No $195k/yr ongoing lease premium
  • ✓Zone sort wins by $1.1M+/yr even before capex advantage
  • ✓High-cap van still has a 60-min loading problem — zone sort has 25 min
  • ✓100% of 10,000 simulations showed a positive saving
What the pilot must confirm
  • →Actual floor sort time (stopwatch, not estimate) — this is the key number
  • →Zone sort load time achievable in ~25 min with zone bin infrastructure
  • →DC sort productivity cost (extra handling at the DC to pre-sort to zones)
  • →Driver compliance — do drivers follow zone sequence or revert to habits?
Even if floor sort is only 60 minutes (not 85), the annual saving is still $1.6M+. The pilot resolves all four questions in 6 weeks at low cost.
