The model — three additive components
1
Walk time
Density-dependent. Shorter in dense urban (30s) and longer in low-density suburban (52s). Derived from Poisson nearest-neighbour walk geometry. Identical for ATL and attended.
2
Handoff (ATL-weighted)
ATL: scan + place parcel + photo (much shorter). Attended: door wait + interaction. Fleet blend = ATL% × ATL handoff + (1−ATL%) × attended handoff.
3
Per-package increment
8.7–12s per additional package at the same stop. Linear model. Grounded in Winkenbach (2021) log-coefficient derivation for door delivery.

Six Delivery Topology Cells

Service time is modelled across a 2×3 grid. The two dimensions — geography band and stop type — are defined precisely below. Each cell is a distinct service time regime with its own density reference, handoff distribution, and ATL profile.

Dimension 1 — Geography Band

Defined by stop density (stops/km²) and built-environment characteristics.

CBD
CBD / Urban Dense
Density ≥ 2,000 stops/km² (ref. 2,500–4,000)
  • ▸High-density urban core — typically within 3–5 km of the CBD
  • ▸Multi-storey mixed-use buildings; minimal parking adjacent to door
  • ▸Driver typically parks on arterial street and walks to multiple stops per block
  • ▸Short walk times (30–34s) but complex building access raises handoff
  • ▸AU examples: Sydney CBD, Darlinghurst, Surry Hills, Waterloo, Alexandria
SUB
Suburban / Fringe
Density 800–1,500 stops/km² (ref. 800–1,500)
  • ▸Residential suburbs and strip-retail precincts beyond the urban core
  • ▸Predominantly detached or low-rise dwellings; street-level parking available
  • ▸Driver typically parks directly in front of or adjacent to the stop
  • ▸Longer walk times (41–52s) but simpler door access reduces handoff
  • ▸AU examples: Bondi, Maroubra, Kensington, Glebe, Rose Bay, Paddington

Dimension 2 — Stop Type

Defined by the physical nature of the delivery interaction at the door.

SFD
Residential SFD
Definition

Single-family dwelling or townhouse with direct street-level door access. No shared building entry or common areas. Includes freestanding houses, semi-detached, and row terrace.

ATL profile

High ATL compatibility — safe-drop location (porch, letterbox area, side passage) typically available. ATL handoff very short: scan + place + photograph (≈35–75s).

MDU
MDU / Apartment
Definition

Multi-dwelling unit building requiring shared entry. Includes apartments, units, and serviced residences. Driver must navigate intercom, security door, lifts, and internal corridors to reach the individual door.

ATL profile

Partial ATL benefit — building navigation is unavoidable even for ATL deliveries. ATL saving is on door-wait only; building access time (buzzing in, lift, corridor) remains.

COM
Commercial
Definition

Business premises — retail, office, hospitality, or industrial. Includes reception-managed buildings, street-front shops, loading docks, and service entrances. Business hours constrain delivery windows.

ATL profile

Lower ATL applicability — most commercial stops require confirmation of receipt or proof of identity. ATL mainly applies to simple strip-retail drop-offs.

ATL Rate Sensitivity

Authority-to-Leave (ATL) dramatically reduces the handoff component by eliminating the door-wait and recipient interaction. The fleet ATL rate is the single most impactful lever on average service time.

AT
Attended delivery
Recipient must be present
  • • Knock / ring doorbell
  • • Wait for resident to answer (variable — 15–90s)
  • • Present parcel, confirm recipient
  • • Scan and obtain acknowledgement
  • • If no answer: re-attempt card, return to depot
Handoff range: 55–280s depending on topology. Oslo empirical: 199s handoff at residential.
ATL
Authority to Leave
Pre-authorised safe drop
  • • Walk to nominated safe location
  • • Place parcel (no recipient interaction required)
  • • Scan + photograph for proof of delivery
  • • No wait time — immediate return to vehicle
  • • No failed delivery re-attempt cost
Handoff range: 45–210s depending on topology. Oslo empirical: 78s handoff ATL residential.

Model Specification

Three additive components. ATL rate governs the handoff blend.

Full model equation

T(n, δ, c, α, σ) =
  Twalk(δ)
  + [α × Thandoff,ATL(c) + (1−α) × Thandoff,att(c)]
  + (n−1) × βc
  + σ × 45
where:
  α = ATL rate (0–1), fleet assumption = 0.85
  δ = stop density (stops/km²)
  c = topology cell (C1–C6)
  β = increment per additional pkg (s)
  σ = 1 if signature required, else 0

Walk time — Poisson nearest-neighbour

d̄walk = 1.35 × 500 / √δ [metres, one-way]
Twalk(δ) = 2 × d̄walk / 1.2 + 12 [seconds]

Distributional form

Thandoff ~ LogNormal(μln, σln)
μln = ln(μ) − ½σln²
σln = √[ln(1 + (σ/μ)²)]
Why log-normal? Service time is strictly positive, right-skewed, and multiplicatively influenced by independent factors (access difficulty, recipient behaviour, package size). Formal distributional testing across the 10-study corpus consistently favoured log-normal over exponential, gamma, or normal.

Appendix A — Research Methodology

How This Data Was Created

A complete account of the search strategy, extraction protocol, meta-analysis procedures, and calibration decisions that produced the service time estimates in this paper.

A1

Research Design

Study type

Systematic literature review with quantitative meta-analysis. The review follows a modified PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) protocol, adapted for operational logistics data where controlled experimental conditions are rare. All included studies report empirical service time measurements from live delivery operations — no simulation or theoretical papers were included.

Objective

To establish literature-grounded probability distributions for per-stop service time across six delivery topology cells relevant to Australian last-mile networks. The primary output is a parameterised three-component model — walk time, handoff time, and per-package increment — that can be used as a Monte Carlo input distribution for route-time and cost modelling.

A3

Inclusion & Exclusion Criteria

Included ✓
  • ✓Studies reporting mean service time per stop from live operations
  • ✓GPS trace studies, carrier operational data, field observation studies
  • ✓Studies measuring courier/driver time (not customer collection time)
  • ✓B2B freight studies where B2C calibration could be defensibly applied
  • ✓Studies measuring either attended or unattended (ATL) delivery modes
Excluded ✗
  • ✗Simulation or modelling studies with no empirical field component
  • ✗Studies measuring customer dwell time or collection time (not driver time)
  • ✗Locker-facilitated deliveries (removed in v2.1 — locker infrastructure changes the stop fundamentally)
  • ✗Studies with n < 30 stops (insufficient for distributional fitting)
  • ✗Express/same-day studies where service time is not representative of standard B2C operations
A4

Data Extraction Protocol

For each included study, the following fields were extracted into a structured CSV (service_time_extraction_v2.csv):

Service time definition

Whether the reported figure covers total stop time, handoff only, walk only, or curb dwell. Studies reporting different definitions were decomposed before pooling.

Central tendency & spread

Mean, median, standard deviation, and percentiles extracted where available. Where only median was reported, mean was estimated using the log-normal relationship (mean ≈ median × exp(σ²/2)).

Topology classification

Each study's study geography was mapped to one or more of the six cells based on stop density, stop type description, and urban form descriptors in the paper.

Delivery mode

Whether measurements reflect attended delivery, unattended/ATL, or a mix. Studies with mixed modes were split by proportion where disclosed, otherwise treated as attended.

B2C applicability flag

Each study was flagged as: Pure B2C (no adjustment), Mixed B2B/B2C (×0.50), or B2B freight (×0.40). The adjustment was applied to mean and SD before pooling.

Quality score (1–5)

Scored on: GPS validation (2pts), sample size ≥500 (1pt), full distribution reported (1pt), peer-reviewed (1pt). Scores used to weight credibility discussions but not pooling weights (those use SE).

A5

Meta-Analysis Procedure

Pooling method

Within each topology cell, available studies were pooled using inverse-variance weighted (IVW) averaging:

μ_pool = Σ(wᵢ × μᵢ) / Σwᵢ
wᵢ = 1 / SEᵢ²
SEᵢ = SDᵢ / √nᵢ

Where SD was not reported, it was imputed at 45% of the mean (the median CV across studies where SD was available).

Heterogeneity (I²)

Cochran's Q statistic and I² were computed for each cell with ≥2 studies:

Q = Σwᵢ(μᵢ − μ_pool)²
I² = max(0, (Q − (k−1)) / Q × 100)

All cells with multiple studies showed I² > 88%. This was interpreted as genuine cross-country heterogeneity (not error), resulting from differences in urban form, driver culture, and network design. The pooled mean is used as a calibration centre; the full study range informs Monte Carlo bounds.

Log-normal parameter derivation

Once a pooled mean (μ) and pooled SD (σ) were established per cell, log-normal parameters were derived analytically:

σ_ln = √[ln(1 + (σ/μ)²)]
μ_ln = ln(μ) − ½σ_ln²

For cells with zero or one study, parameters were set by synthetic prior: a calibrated prior informed by the closest analogous cell in the literature corpus, scaled for the cell's density reference and stop-type classification. Synthetic cells are flagged in all outputs (C1, C5, C6).

A6

Walk Time Model

Theoretical basis

Walk time between the driver's parked vehicle and the stop door was modelled using the Poisson nearest-neighbour distance formula. Under Poisson-distributed stops at density δ, the expected nearest-neighbour distance is:

d̄ = 1/(2√δ) metres (theoretical)
d̄_walk = 1.35 × 500/√δ metres (calibrated)
T_walk = 2 × d̄_walk / 1.2 + 12 seconds
Calibration decisions
  • ▸Factor 1.35: empirical correction for non-Manhattan street geometry (diagonal detours, blocked footpaths)
  • ▸Walk speed 1.2 m/s: loaded-carrier walking pace (Dalla Chiara 2025)
  • ▸Fixed 12s: vehicle exit/entry overhead (door, step down, lock)
  • ▸Walk SD = 35% of mean to reflect parking position variability
  • ▸Cross-validated against Gevaers (2014): model 77s at ~300/km² vs implied 105s — directionally correct
A7

ATL Calibration Methodology

Empirical anchor

Kjelle et al. (2023) is the only study in the corpus that directly compares attended and ATL (unattended) service time at the same residential delivery network. Measured at ~1,500 stops/km² (Oslo):

ATL total: 119s (walk ~41s + handoff ~78s)
Attended total: 240s (walk ~41s + handoff ~199s)
ATL handoff fraction: 78/199 = 39%
Cell-by-cell ATL handoff
  • C4:75s — Oslo ATL handoff 78s × AU adj 0.95. Best-evidenced ATL estimate.
  • C1:55s — CBD residential analogue; slightly complex access but no building navigation.
  • C2:160s — Building navigation unavoidable. ATL saves door-wait only (~30% reduction).
  • C3:210s — Commercial ATL reduces interaction but retains building/reception navigation.
  • C5:130s — Suburban MDU, simpler building access. ~35% reduction vs attended.
  • C6:45s — Strip retail, quick kerb-side placement. ~36% reduction vs attended.
Fleet ATL rate assumption

An 85% fleet ATL rate is applied as the base case for Australian B2C parcel delivery. This reflects typical network-level ATL authorisation rates observed in Australian domestic parcels (where customers pre-authorise safe-drop at account or consignment level). The rate varies by geography: suburban residential carries the highest ATL rate (>90%), CBD apartment and commercial stops carry lower effective rates (~70–75%) due to building access restrictions. The 85% figure represents a network-weighted average. All interactive tools allow this assumption to be varied from 0–100%.

A8

Australian Calibration Factors

No peer-reviewed Australian study of driver-side service time exists. All primary studies are from the United States, Norway, Belgium, and Chile. Australian calibration factors were derived by comparing urban form characteristics, building typologies, and network density between the study locations and their Australian counterparts:

A9

Stop-to-Cell Assignment Rules

Operational stops are assigned to a topology cell using two fields from the stop master record: stop_type and suburb. The assignment logic is:

# Assignment pseudo-code
if suburb in CBD_SUBURBS:
  if stop_type == "apartment": → C2 (Urban MDU)
  if stop_type == "business": → C3 (CBD Commercial)
  else: → C1 (CBD Residential)
else:
  if stop_type == "apartment": → C5 (Suburban MDU)
  if stop_type == "business": → C6 (Suburban Commercial)
  else: → C4 (Suburban Residential)
CBD suburb list The CBD classification is based on suburb boundaries, not a density calculation at runtime. Suburbs are pre-classified as CBD or Suburban in a reference table. This allows the assignment to run without GIS infrastructure at the point of routing.
Density is a cell property, not a stop property Each cell carries a reference density (δ_ref) used to compute walk time. The actual stop density for a given run can be passed as an override to the walk time model — particularly useful for high-density routes where multiple parcels are delivered per block.
A10

Limitations & Future Work

No AU empirical data

The entire model rests on international data with AU calibration factors. The factors are judgement-based. AU field measurement is the highest-priority data gap.

High I² across cells

I² > 88% reflects genuine heterogeneity but means the pooled mean is a weak estimator. The model should be used as a planning centre estimate with wide uncertainty bounds.

C1, C5, C6 synthetic

Three cells have no literature studies — parameters are synthetic priors. These cells represent a significant fraction of suburban volumes and warrant priority field measurement.

ATL rate assumed

The 85% fleet ATL assumption is unvalidated for the specific network. Carrier operational data (scan-type codes: ATL vs attended) would directly measure this parameter.

Linear increment model

The β per-package increment is modelled as linear. In practice, marginal time per package likely decreases at high n (economies of handling). This warrants validation at n > 5.

Season / time-of-day effects

All estimates represent annual averages. Peak season (November–December) likely adds 15–20% to residential attended handoff due to gift packaging and recipient interaction. No seasonal model is currently included.
